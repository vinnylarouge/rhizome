> **Before trusting a vivid specific story, anchor on how often this kind of thing actually happens.**

## When it fires
- A compelling individual narrative tempts you to ignore the wider population.
- You're forecasting a project, startup, diagnosis, or relationship's outcome.
- The inside story feels unique and exempt from statistics.

## Questions it forces
- What reference class does this case belong to, and what's the base rate there?
- How much should the specific evidence move me off that prior?

## What to do
Start from the base rate of the reference class, then adjust for genuinely diagnostic specifics — don't start from the specifics and forget the prior. When the base rate is grim, demand strong evidence before believing you're the exception.

## Failure mode
A poorly chosen reference class is worse than none; and base rates can be over-applied to genuinely novel situations where no good reference class exists.

## Out-of-domain example
A founder pitches "this time is different," but the base rate of startups in the category surviving five years is 10% — the burden is to show, concretely, why they beat it.
