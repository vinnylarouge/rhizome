> **The higher the stakes on a social indicator, the more it corrupts and distorts the very process it was meant to monitor.**

## When it fires
- A single quantitative indicator is tied to high-stakes consequences for people (funding, jobs, rankings).
- The measured process itself starts visibly deforming around the metric.
- Evaluation, accountability, or compliance regimes are being designed.

## Questions it forces
- What perverse behaviour does attaching real stakes to this number reward?
- Is the process bending to serve the indicator instead of the goal?

## What to do
Lower the stakes on any single indicator, decouple measurement from punishment where you can, and monitor for distortion directly. Prefer a basket of hard-to-game signals plus human judgement over one decisive number.

## Failure mode
Can become an excuse to abandon accountability entirely. Some indicators under moderate stakes remain informative; the law is about *high-stakes* pressure, not all measurement.

## Out-of-domain example
Teaching to the test: when school funding rides on standardised scores, curricula narrow and scores rise while actual learning stagnates — the indicator ate the process.
