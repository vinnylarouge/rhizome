> **The boundary of what you can reliably evaluate matters more than how large that area is.**

## When it fires
- A seductive opportunity sits in an expert-coded domain you don't really understand.
- You're borrowing someone else's confidence rather than holding your own.
- An unusually high promised return tempts you outside familiar ground.

## Questions it forces
- Can I actually evaluate this, or am I relying on others' conviction?
- Where exactly is the edge of my competence here?

## What to do
Know your boundary and act inside it: pass, partner with someone who has the competence, learn deliberately before entering, or shrink your stake to match your real understanding. Expanding the circle is fine — but do it on purpose, not under temptation.

## Failure mode
Hardens into cowardice or permanent narrowness if never deliberately expanded. The point is to know the boundary, not to worship it.

## Out-of-domain example
A brilliant mathematician founding a biotech must separate "I understand the optimisation story" from "I understand clinical, regulatory, and biological risk" — competence in one is not competence in the other.
