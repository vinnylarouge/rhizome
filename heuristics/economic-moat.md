> **Prefer positions defended by a durable barrier competitors cannot cheaply cross.**

## When it fires
- You're evaluating a strategy, business, career, or institution for *longevity*, not just current strength.
- Profits or advantages are high enough to attract imitators.
- You must distinguish a temporary lead from a defensible one.

## Questions it forces
- Why can't a well-funded competitor simply copy this next year?
- Is the advantage structural (network effects, switching costs, scale, brand, regulation) or merely a head start?

## What to do
Build or buy advantages with self-reinforcing barriers — network effects, high switching costs, economies of scale, hard-won trust — and keep widening the moat, because every durable advantage is under constant erosion.

## Failure mode
Moats lull. They can be dissolved overnight by a technology shift that changes the cost frontier; yesterday's fortress (a taxi medallion, a newspaper's classifieds) becomes worthless.

## Out-of-domain example
An academic field with a unique dataset and tacit methods has a moat: rivals can read the papers but can't quickly reproduce the apparatus or judgement, so the lab compounds its lead.
