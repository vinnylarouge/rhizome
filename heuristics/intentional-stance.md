> **Predict a system by choosing the cheapest useful stance: treat it as an agent with goals, as a designed mechanism, or as raw physics.**

## When it fires
- You need to predict a complex system's behaviour and exact mechanism is intractable.
- You're unsure whether to model something as "wanting" things or as "built to" do things.
- Anthropomorphising is either tempting or being wrongly forbidden.

## Questions it forces
- Which stance predicts this system's behaviour most economically — intentional, design, or physical?
- Am I treating a useful predictive stance as a claim about what's *really* inside?

## What to do
Pick the stance that buys the most prediction for the least detail: ascribe beliefs/goals when that forecasts well (intentional), shift to function when you care about *how* (design), drop to physics only when you must. Keep the stance as a tool, not a metaphysical commitment.

## Failure mode
Mistaking the intentional stance for literal psychology over-attributes mind (to thermostats, markets, models); refusing it where it works forfeits cheap, accurate prediction.

## Out-of-domain example
Treating a chess engine as "trying to control the centre" predicts its moves far faster than simulating its search — even though it has no desires. The stance is instrumentally true.
