> **Push a system at equilibrium and it shifts to counteract you — the harder you push, the more it pushes back.**

## When it fires
- An intervention provokes a compensating reaction that erodes its effect.
- A reform "works" briefly, then the old equilibrium quietly reasserts itself.
- You're surprised that a forced change produced an opposite second-order response.

## Questions it forces
- What restoring force will this push activate?
- Where will the displaced "pressure" re-emerge?

## What to do
Anticipate the counter-reaction before acting: either pre-empt the restoring force, change the system's equilibrium *set point* rather than fighting it directly, or accept that sustained pressure is needed to hold a new state. Don't mistake the rebound for failure of the idea.

## Failure mode
Not every system has a strong restoring force; some changes lock in (see hysteresis). Over-applying the principle breeds fatalism about whether change is possible at all.

## Out-of-domain example
Add a lane to a congested highway and induced demand fills it: the transport system relaxes back toward its prior travel-time equilibrium, defeating the "obvious" fix.
