> **The strongest places to change a system are its goals and paradigms; tweaking parameters is the weakest.**

## When it fires
- You've been adjusting numbers (budgets, rates, taxes) and nothing fundamental shifts.
- A system stubbornly reproduces the same outcomes despite reform.
- You need to find *where* to push, not just how hard.

## Questions it forces
- Am I intervening at a parameter (weak) or at a rule, goal, or paradigm (strong)?
- What is the highest leverage point I can actually reach?

## What to do
Climb Meadows' ladder: above numbers sit buffers and flows, then feedback loops, then rules and information flows, then the system's *goal*, and finally the *paradigm* it springs from. Aim as high as you can act — but note high-leverage points are the hardest to move and easiest to get backwards.

## Failure mode
High-leverage interventions are also high-variance: push a paradigm the wrong way and you do great harm. Leverage cuts both directions.

## Out-of-domain example
A company chasing quarterly targets (a parameter) stays toxic until it changes the *goal* it optimises — say, from share price to customer retention. The goal shift reorganises everything beneath it.
