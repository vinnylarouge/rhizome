> **The knowledge needed to decide well is dispersed, tacit, and local — so whoever is far from it cannot decide well, however smart.**

## When it fires
- A central authority is making decisions that depend on facts only the people on the ground hold.
- A plan looks optimal on paper but the operators keep "working around" it.
- You are tempted to standardise a process whose value lives in case-by-case judgement.

## Questions it forces
- Who actually holds the relevant information — and is it the same person who decides?
- What is being lost in the round-trip from the edge to the centre and back?

## What to do
Push the decision down to where the information already is. Where you must centralise, build a price-like signal that aggregates dispersed knowledge instead of demanding everyone report raw facts upward. Reward edge actors for acting on what they know, not for compliance.

## Failure mode
Localisation without coordination produces incoherence; some knowledge genuinely is global (systemic risk, externalities) and belongs at the centre. Don't decentralise the things that only make sense whole.

## Out-of-domain example
A foundation model team tuning prompts from headquarters cannot see the failure modes that frontline annotators hit daily. Give annotators authority to amend the rubric, not just a box to file complaints into.
