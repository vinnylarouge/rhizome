> **Demand enough buffer between your estimate and the cliff that being wrong doesn't ruin you.**

## When it fires
- A decision rests on an uncertain estimate (a valuation, a load rating, a deadline).
- The downside of being wrong is large or irreversible.
- Everything is priced for perfection and you're tempted to assume it.

## Questions it forces
- How wrong can my estimate be before this breaks?
- Is the gap between my estimate and the failure point large enough to survive that error?

## What to do
Size the buffer to your *uncertainty*, not your point estimate: build bridges to hold far more than the rated load, buy at a price well below appraised value, set deadlines with slack. The more uncertain the estimate, the wider the margin.

## Failure mode
Excessive margin is its own cost — over-engineering, capital left idle, opportunities passed. The buffer should scale with uncertainty and stakes, not be maximal everywhere.

## Out-of-domain example
A surgeon schedules three hours for a two-hour operation: the margin absorbs the complications that turn an on-time plan into a fatal overrun.
