> **The practical, situated know-how that makes things actually work cannot be written down without losing exactly what made it valuable.**

## When it fires
- A task succeeds because of experienced judgement, feel, or rules-of-thumb that no manual captures.
- You are about to replace a veteran with a documented procedure and expect equal output.
- The official process and the way work really gets done have quietly diverged.

## Questions it forces
- What does the expert do that they cannot fully articulate?
- If I codify this, what irreducible judgement will the codified version silently drop?

## What to do
Protect and transmit metis through apprenticeship, not just documentation: pair novices with experts, preserve slack for improvisation, and treat workarounds as data about where the formal system is wrong. Codify the stable 80%, but keep a human with discretion on the residual.

## Failure mode
Metis can ossify into superstition or guild gatekeeping. Not every undocumented practice is wisdom — some is just habit that a good standard would rightly kill.

## Out-of-domain example
A senior on-call engineer "knows" which alerts are noise. Replace them with a rigid runbook and incident response gets slower, because the runbook can't encode the pattern-recognition that triage really runs on.
