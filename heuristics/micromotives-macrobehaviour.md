> **Mild individual preferences can aggregate into extreme collective patterns nobody intended or wants.**

## When it fires
- A stark global pattern (segregation, clustering, a norm) is being read as evidence of strong individual intent.
- You're inferring people's preferences from an aggregate outcome.
- A system self-organises into a state no participant chose.

## Questions it forces
- What *weak* local rule could generate this strong global pattern?
- Am I mistaking an emergent aggregate for a deliberate collective preference?

## What to do
Model the micro-rule and simulate the aggregate before attributing intent: a small bias, iterated across many agents and a threshold, can tip into a dramatic macrostructure. To change the pattern, change the local incentive or the threshold, not people's hearts.

## Failure mode
Not every macro-pattern is emergent from benign micromotives — some really are driven by strong preferences or coercion. Assuming emergence can excuse real malice.

## Out-of-domain example
Schelling's segregation model: agents who merely prefer not to be a small minority (happy at 30% same-type) still sort into near-total separation — a vivid gap between micromotive and macro-outcome.
