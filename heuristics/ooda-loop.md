> **Whoever cycles observe–orient–decide–act faster forces the opponent to react to a reality that no longer exists.**

## When it fires
- You're in active competition against an adapting adversary.
- A rival is out-manoeuvring you despite your having more resources.
- Speed of adaptation, not size, seems to decide outcomes.

## Questions it forces
- How fast is my observe-orient-decide-act cycle versus theirs?
- Is *orientation* — the mental model I interpret reality through — my real bottleneck?

## What to do
Tighten your loop and disrupt the opponent's: act faster than they can re-orient, so they're always responding to your last move. Boyd's emphasis: orientation is the schwerpunkt — invest most in the model through which you see, because a fast loop pointed by a wrong model just loses faster.

## Failure mode
Speed without sound orientation is frantic error; and not every situation is adversarial — looping fast against a stable environment just adds churn (see delayed feedback).

## Out-of-domain example
A startup shipping and learning weekly out-adapts an incumbent on an annual planning cycle: by the time the incumbent responds, the startup has already moved twice.
