> **Shared resources can be governed sustainably without privatisation or the state — given the right locally-designed rules.**

## When it fires
- A commons is being overused and the only options on the table are privatise or nationalise.
- A community shares something (compute, code, a space, norms) and needs durable rules.
- Top-down control keeps failing against local realities.

## Questions it forces
- Who has clear rights, who monitors, who sanctions, and who can change the rules?
- Are the people affected the ones designing the rules?

## What to do
Apply Ostrom's principles: clear boundaries on resource and membership, rules matched to local conditions, participation by the affected in rule-making, monitoring by the users themselves, graduated sanctions, cheap conflict resolution, and nested layers for large commons. Design governance; don't rely on goodwill or distant authority.

## Failure mode
Principles tuned for bounded communities can fail under anonymity, scale, or hostile capture without stronger institutions. They describe necessary features, not a guaranteed recipe.

## Out-of-domain example
An open-source project survives free-riding through clear contributor roles, maintainer monitoring, graduated responses (review → revert → ban), and a governance doc — Ostrom's principles in code form.
