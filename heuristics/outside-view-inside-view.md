> **Forecast first from the reference class (outside), then adjust with case-specific mechanism (inside) — not the reverse.**

## When it fires
- Estimating a timeline, budget, or success probability for a specific plan.
- The inside story is detailed and persuasive and you're tempted to trust it alone.
- Past similar efforts exist to compare against.

## Questions it forces
- How did *similar* projects actually turn out (the outside view)?
- Does my mechanistic inside story justify deviating from that, and by how much?

## What to do
Anchor on the distribution of outcomes for comparable cases, then move off it only as far as genuinely diagnostic specifics warrant. The inside view supplies mechanism and detail; the outside view supplies calibration. Use both, in that order.

## Failure mode
Pure outside view ignores real, knowable particulars; pure inside view breeds the planning fallacy. Over-weighting either is the error.

## Out-of-domain example
"Our kitchen remodel will take six weeks" (inside view, every step planned) versus "remodels like this average eleven weeks" (outside view). The honest estimate starts at eleven and argues down.
