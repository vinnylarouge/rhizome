> **A system pushes back against naive intervention because each actor pulls the stock toward its own goal.**

## When it fires
- A reform is fought to a standstill and the problem snaps back.
- Multiple stakeholders each resist a change that thwarts their separate goals.
- Effort scales up but net movement stays near zero.

## Questions it forces
- Whose goals does the current state already satisfy, and how will they resist?
- What feedback loop restores the old equilibrium after I act?

## What to do
Find the goals pulling against yours and either align incentives so everyone gains, or change the shared goal rather than overpowering resistance. Bring the actors into the redesign; harmonised goals dissolve resistance that brute force only stiffens.

## Failure mode
Sometimes resistance signals that the intervention is genuinely wrong, not merely opposed. Don't read all pushback as an obstacle to crush — it may be information.

## Out-of-domain example
A crackdown on one drug shifts demand to a more dangerous substitute: suppliers and users re-route around the intervention, and the system's harm level barely moves.
