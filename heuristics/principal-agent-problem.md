> **A delegate acts on their own incentives and private information, not yours — so good delegation is incentive design.**

## When it fires
- You delegate a task to someone whose interests differ from yours.
- The agent knows things you can't observe (hidden information or hidden action).
- Outcomes disappoint and "they just need to try harder" is the proposed fix.

## Questions it forces
- What does the agent actually gain or lose from each outcome?
- What can they do that I cannot observe, and how will they use that gap?

## What to do
Align incentives rather than rely on goodwill: tie reward to verifiable outcomes, reduce information asymmetry with monitoring, and accept residual agency cost as the price of delegation. Beware that any metric you reward will be optimised — sometimes against you.

## Failure mode
Over-monitoring destroys trust and intrinsic motivation, and incentive schemes invite gaming (Goodhart). Some relationships run better on professional norms than on contracts.

## Out-of-domain example
A real-estate agent paid a percentage has weak incentive to squeeze the last 2% from a sale — the extra effort costs them a lot and earns them little, so they push you to accept early.
