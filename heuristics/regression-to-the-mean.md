> **Extreme observations are partly luck, so the next measurement tends to drift back toward average — no cause required.**

## When it fires
- A record-breaking result is followed by a more ordinary one and people credit (or blame) an intervention.
- You're evaluating whether a treatment "worked" on cases selected for being extreme.
- A streak, a star year, or a crisis is being explained by a story.

## Questions it forces
- How much of this extreme value was noise that will simply not repeat?
- Would the apparent effect appear even if my intervention did nothing?

## What to do
Expect the extreme to moderate on its own and build that into the baseline before crediting any cause. To measure a real effect on selected-extreme cases, use a control group — otherwise regression masquerades as success or failure.

## Failure mode
Not all change is regression; sometimes the extreme is a true signal of a new regime. Reflexively attributing every reversion to noise discards real trends.

## Out-of-domain example
"Being yelled at after a bad performance improves the next one" — but the next one was likely to improve anyway. Praise after a peak seems to hurt for the same illusory reason.
