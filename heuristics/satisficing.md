> **Under real search costs, stop at the first option that clears a "good enough" bar instead of hunting for the optimum.**

## When it fires
- The search space is large and full optimisation would cost more than it's worth.
- Analysis paralysis is setting in over a decision with diminishing returns to deliberation.
- "Best" is ill-defined but "acceptable" is clear.

## Questions it forces
- What is my acceptability threshold (my aspiration level) here?
- Is the cost of further search greater than the expected improvement it buys?

## What to do
Set an aspiration level in advance, take the first option that meets it, and move on. Reserve optimisation for the few decisions where the payoff gradient is steep; satisfice the rest. Adjust the threshold up or down as you learn what's attainable.

## Failure mode
Threshold set too low locks in mediocrity; set too high, you've just renamed optimising. And some decisions (irreversible, high-stakes) genuinely deserve exhaustive search.

## Out-of-domain example
Choosing a restaurant: maximisers research for an hour to find "the best" and enjoy it less than satisficers who pick the first solid option and spend the saved hour eating.

## Related
A search-cost cousin of [[circle-of-competence]] and [[optionality]]; rests on [[near-decomposability]] (you can satisfice a module without solving the whole).
