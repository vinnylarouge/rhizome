> **When people must coordinate without communicating, they converge on whatever option is psychologically salient.**

## When it fires
- Parties need to coordinate but can't (or won't) negotiate explicitly.
- A standard, meeting point, default, or convention has to emerge from nothing.
- Multiple equilibria exist and you need everyone to pick the *same* one.

## Questions it forces
- What option is obvious, salient, or traditional enough that everyone expects everyone else to choose it?
- Can I *create* salience to steer coordination toward the equilibrium I want?

## What to do
Find or manufacture the focal point: use a prominent default, a round number, a shared precedent, or a vivid label so that each party's best guess of others' guesses converges. Salience, not optimality, decides which equilibrium wins.

## Failure mode
Focal points are culturally relative — what's obvious to one group is invisible to another, so cross-cultural coordination misfires. And a salient option can be a *bad* equilibrium that's merely hard to escape (lock-in).

## Out-of-domain example
Two people told to meet in New York "tomorrow" with no further details often name noon at Grand Central — not the optimal spot, just the salient one everyone expects everyone to expect.
