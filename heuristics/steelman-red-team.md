> **Strengthen a claim to its best form and then attack it as hard as you can; what survives is worth believing.**

## When it fires
- A plan or argument feels obviously right and meets no resistance.
- A decision is high-stakes and you've only built the case *for* it.
- Group consensus has formed suspiciously fast.

## Questions it forces
- What is the strongest version of the opposing case (the steelman)?
- If this fails, what will most likely have killed it (the pre-mortem)?

## What to do
First steelman the opposition so you're not beating a strawman, then assign someone to genuinely try to break your plan. Run a pre-mortem: imagine the failure has happened and explain why. Adopt only what survives a real attack.

## Failure mode
Performative red-teaming — going through the motions without authority to change the decision — gives false confidence. And endless adversarial review can stall action that should just be taken.

## Out-of-domain example
Before launching a product, a team writes the imagined post-mortem of its failure; "users never understood the onboarding" surfaces months early, while it's still cheap to fix.
