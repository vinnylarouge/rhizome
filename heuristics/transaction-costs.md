> **A firm exists wherever coordinating inside it is cheaper than transacting in the market — the boundary follows the costs.**

## When it fires
- A build-versus-buy, in-house-versus-outsource, or merge-versus-partner decision.
- You're drawing the boundary of a team, product, or organisation.
- A technology change has shifted the cost of coordinating or contracting.

## Questions it forces
- Is it cheaper to do this inside the boundary or to buy it across a market interface?
- Which transaction costs — search, negotiation, enforcement — dominate here?

## What to do
Put inside the boundary the activities whose market transaction costs (search, bargaining, monitoring, enforcement) exceed the cost of internal coordination; buy the rest. Re-evaluate when technology changes those costs — falling coordination costs shrink firms, falling contracting costs grow markets.

## Failure mode
Transaction costs are hard to measure, so the principle explains boundaries better in hindsight than it predicts them. Internalising too much rebuilds bureaucracy; externalising too much creates fragile dependency chains.

## Out-of-domain example
Cheap platforms and APIs lowered contracting costs, so many startups now "rent" payments, hosting, and auth rather than build them — the efficient firm boundary moved outward into the market.
